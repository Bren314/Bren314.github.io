Research

High Redshift Galaxies

My current research focuses on galaxies that are around and less than a billion years after the Big Bang (z>4.5). This region of time is known as the Epoch of Reionization, which is likely the time the first stars and galaxies are forming. Galaxies at this redshift were up until recently outside of our abilities to view. As shown in this gif the spectra of galaxies gets shifted to infrared wavelengths as the redshift increases. However, with the launch of JWST we can now view and study these galaxies to learn how our universe formed. 

Gif made by Taylor Hutchison

Galaxy Evolution & Interstellar Medium

With the lauch of the JWST we are able to access the high redshift universe, giving us a several new puzzle pieces to show how galaxies changed overtime. The figure below highlights the regions of time I have worked with and studied the changes in galaxies emission-lines, stellar mass, star formation rate. I compare this to studying the different life stages of human devoplment. How a baby interacts with the world around it is different than that of an adult or even toddler. Similarly, early galaxies interact internally and externally with the universe is different. At each time period the universe and galaxies have different properties that feed off of each other. 

I have also studied the change in a galaxies interstellar medium conditions. The interstellar medium is the region of space between stars within a galaxy, which is a mixure of gas and dust. This region of space is affected by stars, active galactic nuclei, and shocks which can deplete or replenish gas which determines the lifespan of active star formation. Due of the interstellar mediums ability to interact with the stellar objects within it, it can provide a great overview picture of the entire galaxy.

Shaping the Universe

At these high redshifts we have found that our previous methods of extracting information from the spectra have been shown to be ineffective or are still on the fence. One method that shows promise is the use of broad hydrogen features to note Active Galactic Nuclei. I am putting together several methods of classifing galaxies which are Star Formaring from galaxies with an Active Galactic Nuclei and outlining the best emission-line method to find Active Galactic Nuclei at z>4.5

Studying the intergalactic medium is key to understanding the formation and evolution of galaxies in the early Universe. Therefore to understand the difference in contribution between galaxies and Active Galactic Nuclei, the galactic properties of both should be related to Lyman-alpha. This is work I plan to do with my upcoming paper with my GMOS observations.

Emission-Line Diagnostics

The Emission Lines in a galaxy's spectra gives us a wealth of information about it. The light from these galaxies can tell us the galaxies stellar mass, star formation rate, the conditions of the interstellar medium (metallicity, pessure, and ionization), and whether the galaxy holds an active galactic nuclei. One way we can extract this information from the spectra is from plotting emission-line ratios. Here is the OHNO diagram I defined previous to aid in our understanding of galaxies at Cosmic Noon (z~2), which is denoted as the time star formation was at its peak.

Emission Line Grism

The JWST has a massive improvement from Hubble for Grism observations. JWST has two dispersions of light, whereas Hubble only had one. As seen here this is very useful in decontaminating a galaxy's spectra from other galaxies, as galaxies overlapping in one dispersion are seperate in another. This in combination with NIRCam Grism observing  everything in its field of view means we can observe a more unbiased and complete view of our universe. 

I have compared NIRCam Grism emission-line measurements to those from NIRSpec MSA to indicate slitloss and sample comparision. I have also used Grism and NIRSpec MSA to look at the interstellar medium condiation of galaxies z>4. These Grism galaxies will also be able to be spatially resoved allowing future work to study regions of galaxies up to z~6.5.

Spatially Resolved Galaxies

      The next natural step to understanding galaxies is to start breaking them down into regions, the previous work above just looks at the galaxy as a whole. Whereas here we can start discovering the sources of what we are finding. For example, the emission line of the entire galaxy may indicate a high ionization. However, the source of that ionization depends on where it is seen in the galaxy. If we break a galaxy into inner and outer regions shown in the left panel above, we could see if that ionization is primarily from the inner region of the galaxy (indicated an Active Galactic Nuclei)  or in the outer region (indicating a gas flow or an off-nuclear Active Galactic Nuclei). In the case above because the high ionization in the outer region (dashed black line in right panel) is different from the high continuum light region (arrow in right panel). This indicates the galaxy has a gas flow.
